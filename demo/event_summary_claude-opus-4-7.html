Single discovery
Among the 42 faculty who described a use case in the registration survey, only ~14 % named a teaching problem — yet Track A (Teaching) is the track with the deepest prepared infrastructure (ungraded exam PDFs, a Mastery Learning Loop skill, human-graded reference set); meanwhile ~52 % described problems that fall under Track C (admin, knowledge, lab automation), which is also the only track that ships without a fixed tutorial. The room with the highest demand has the lowest scaffolding.

What people expect

Inferred from the program, prep materials, and 42 free-text survey responses.

To leave with a working thing, not a tutorial.

The day's centrepiece is a 16:00 live-demo block — one demo per sub-team. Sub-teams are told they "converge on a demoable result", not "complete the exercise". Faculty arrive with named projects (MEMEX, lab monitoring, eISP filler) and want something they can show colleagues on Wednesday.

To compare Claude, Codex, and Antigravity in anger.

Participant instructions require Claude Desktop, Claude Code, and (if no Claude sub) Codex; Antigravity is "useful in Track B if you already have a workflow built around it". Many participants want a side-by-side feel for which CLI works for their workflow.

To understand the agent loop well enough to teach it.

Multiple respondents (#27, #37) say they're attending to prepare a course or department retreat on AI. The plenary's "Agents = Brain + Memory + Tools + Loop" framing is engineered exactly for that hand-off — and that's why the slogan is the loop itself.

Reasoning.
  • Track C (~19). Highest stated demand. ~52% of described problems (eISP, scheduling, RAG over regulations, lab monitoring, credit-transfer matching, travel claims, budget predictions, hardware automation) are Track C territory. Applied Physics dominates the dept distribution and their lab-automation needs anchor here.
  • Track B (~15). Second-largest by stated demand (~28%), but punching above its survey weight because the prepared sub-teams — HPC workflow, agentic debugging, scientific postprocessing — directly match the deepest-described problems (Andrea Volpato's MEMEX, the Nearl refactor, NAISS cluster orchestration, MD-trajectory postprocessing).
  • Track A (~11). Smallest. Only ~14% named a teaching problem, and the math department is the only division that strongly clusters here. Counterweight: Track A has the most demo-ready material, so curiosity walk-ins will push the number up.
  • Confidence: ±3 per track. The "flexible" cohort (10 respondents who left the question blank or said "no specific problem") is the swing vote and will choose by physical proximity — Track A is in Salongen (same room as plenary), so it gets walk-ins of convenience.
